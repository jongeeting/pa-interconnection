Federal Tax Cliff Threatens Pennsylvania's Energy Generation Pipeline

70 Pennsylvania generation projects — 1,583 MW of summer-peak capacity — have already cleared PJM's engineering review and executed Generation Interconnection Agreements. They are stuck on state and local permitting. For the solar and wind subset, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act's federal tax credit deadlines threaten to void project economics within 14 months. Governor Shapiro's HB 502 is the obvious vehicle, but its 25 MW eligibility threshold excludes most of the projects at risk. This is the case for lowering it to 10 MW — or lower.

The case in briefPennsylvania has a permitting bottleneck and a federal countdown clock.

The bottleneck is what makes the policy necessary. The cliff is what makes the timing urgent. HB 502, with the right amendments, addresses both.

1.

The pipeline is real, vetted, and stuck

PJM has already determined these 70 projects can be safely interconnected and signed agreements with each developer. They are not waiting on engineering. They are waiting on bureaucratic process and politics. Most have been in development since 2018–2020.

2.

The federal cliff hits in 14 months

Under OBBBA, solar and wind projects must "begin construction" — meet IRS Notice 2025-42's Physical Work Test — by July 4, 2026, or be fully placed in service by year-end 2027. State and local permits are the binding prerequisite.

3.

HB 502's threshold misses the target

The bill's 25 MW eligibility floor was set as a generic "utility-scale" default. PA's pipeline is overwhelmingly community-scale. Lowering the threshold to 10 MW would expand HB 502's coverage of stuck projects from 29% to 83%.

103 GW authorized → 23 GW built

Per PJM's March 2026 disclosure, of the 103 GW of Generation Interconnection Agreements executed since 2020, only 23 GW reached commercial operation. 77% of GIA-posted projects withdrew before sending power to the grid. Permitting friction is the dominant attrition driver, and the federal cliff makes it worse.

Threshold analysisThe single most consequential amendment to HB 502

Lowering the bill's eligibility threshold expands coverage at every level of the development pipeline. The chart below shows three scopes — all 340 active PA projects, the 70 GIA-posted urgent universe, and the 64 cliff-exposed solar/wind projects — at thresholds from ≥5 MW to ≥100 MW.

Projects covered by HB 502 at each threshold

Three scopes: full active pipeline, GIA-posted, and federal-cliff-exposed solar/wind
The 10 MW line is also defensible on first principles. Below 10 MW, PA solar projects typically operate as net-metered or distributed generation and fall under different regulatory frameworks. Above 10 MW, projects are unambiguously commercial-scale and reasonably subject to a state siting standard. The line is principled, not just convenient.

Counter-argumentsWhat the strongest critiques get right, and where they fall short

Three rebuttals to the case for amending HB 502 are worth taking seriously. None of them undermine the threshold-amendment argument, but each shapes how the policy should be drafted.

"Interconnection, not land use, is the bottleneck"

The MPL Law Firm critique (September 2025) argued that PJM's queue, not local zoning, was historically the binding constraint — and that PA's Municipalities Planning Code already imposes timing limits on local review.

What it gets right: for the 270 active PA projects still in PJM's pre-GIA pipeline, interconnection reform is the bigger lever.

Where it falls short: it doesn't apply to the 70 GIA-posted projects, which have already cleared PJM. For them, state and local permitting is the binding constraint by definition. The MPL critique is best read as "don't let HB 502 be your only reform," not "don't pass HB 502."

"They have GIAs — they'll get built"

It's true that an executed PJM GIA is a meaningful milestone. It's not true that it's a guarantee.

The data: per PJM's March 2026 disclosure, of the 103 GW of GIAs executed across all PJM since 2020, only 23 GW reached commercial operation. 77% of GIA-posted projects withdrew before sending power to the grid.

For PA's 64 cliff-exposed solar and wind projects, the historical attrition rate plus a hard federal deadline 14 months out is a worse setup than the 77% baseline implies. A state policy intervention is the only mechanism available to push the conversion rate higher.

"Local preemption is the wrong tool"

PA League of Cities and PSAB have raised concerns about HB 502's preemption of local land-use controls.

What it gets right: blanket preemption with no environmental safeguards or community-benefit floor would be a real problem.

Where it falls short: the bill as drafted already includes a 90-day RESET Board determination clock, a 45-day public hearing window, and a 180-day Environmental Hearing Board appeal window. The threshold amendment doesn't change any of those safeguards — it just expands which projects qualify for the same procedural framework. The MAREC and PEC testimony record makes the same point.

MethodologyHow this analysis works

Source data

PJM Planning Queues file (legacy queue snapshot, pre-Cycle 1 reform). Obtained via researchers at the University of Pennsylvania ENVS 5100 group through RMI. PA-only export with 1,976 historical and active projects across all fuel types.

Active pipeline definition

Excludes Withdrawn, In Service, and Deactivated projects. Includes Active, Engineering and Procurement, Suspended, Under Construction, and Partially in Service — 340 PA projects, 10,421 MW summer-peak capacity.

GIA-posted urgent universe

Projects whose Generation Interconnection Agreement column shows "Document Posted" — i.e., the agreement has been executed and posted publicly by PJM. 70 PA projects, 1,583 MW. This is the population whose engineering review is complete and whose binding constraint is now state and local permitting.

Federal cliff exposure

Solar and wind projects only. Under OBBBA, these technologies must "begin construction" by July 4, 2026 (per IRS Notice 2025-42's Physical Work Test) or be placed in service by December 31, 2027. Standalone storage and nuclear are on the original IRA timeline through 2033 and are not directly cliff-exposed.

HB 502 threshold

Bill text uses "capacity," interpreted here as PJM's "MW Capacity" (summer net) field. For solar projects this typically runs lower than "MW Energy" (winter net), so the threshold semantics matter. Confirm with bill drafters before final use.

Legislator attribution

Each project is tagged with the State Senate and State House districts whose boundaries cover the project's county under PA's post-2022 redistricting. For projects pinned to county centroids (most), all overlapping districts are listed. Project locations are shown at jittered county centroids since precise lat/lon was not available in the source file. Where parcel-level coordinates are sourced, attribution becomes point-in-polygon and a single Senate + House district per project.

Cycle 1 mix shift

PJM's April 29, 2026 Cycle 1 announcement (811 projects, 220 GW system-wide) showed a dramatic shift in the PJM-wide queue mix: from 66% solar in 2022 to 48% natural gas today. The state-level breakdown of PA projects within Cycle 1 has not yet been published. The site will be refreshed when it is.

GIA-to-COD attrition

PJM's March 2026 disclosure: 103 GW of Generation Interconnection Agreements executed since 2020 produced only 23 GW of commercial operation — a 77% attrition rate. This is the historical baseline. For PA's 64 cliff-exposed solar and wind projects, the OBBBA tax-credit deadlines compress the construction window, making the attrition rate likely to run higher absent state policy intervention.

Limitations. This analysis covers the legacy PJM queue, which closed before April 2026. PJM's Cycle 1 — 811 projects, 220 GW system-wide — will produce a state-level breakdown in coming weeks. When that data lands, we will refresh this analysis to include both the legacy and Cycle 1 PA pipelines. The "stuck on permitting" inference for suspended projects is not yet directly verified; project-level outreach or FOIL requests would confirm.
